AI + Medicine

1. How AI co-clinician could work in practice

Google DeepMind described an AI co-clinician research direction: not an AI doctor replacing the physician, but an AI teammate working under clinical supervision. The key idea is “triadic care”: patient, clinician, and AI agents working together, while the physician keeps final judgment and control.

Practical interpretation: The AI co-clinician is best understood as a supervised clinical reasoning assistant. It can collect information, synthesize evidence, support telemedicine conversations, and flag possible next steps, but the clinician remains responsible for decisions.

What the workflow might look like

Step 1: Patient or clinician asks a question

The input could be a clinical question, medication question, symptom description, or live telemedicine interaction.

Step 2: AI retrieves and checks evidence

The system searches clinical references, summarizes evidence, checks citations, and tries to avoid incorrect or missing information.

Step 3: Clinician reviews and decides

The AI suggests reasoning and possible actions, but the clinician makes the final clinical decision.

Why the architecture matters

For patient-facing conversations, DeepMind describes a safety-oriented dual-agent design: one “Talker” agent interacts with the patient, while a “Planner” module monitors whether the conversation stays within safe clinical boundaries. This is important because medical AI needs guardrails, not just good language ability.

Important limitation: DeepMind explicitly presents this as research, not as a deployed medical product for diagnosis, treatment, or medical advice. The strongest current use case is supervised support, not autonomous clinical care.

My takeaway for our lab

The most interesting lesson is not only about medicine. It is the design pattern: domain expert + AI agents + evidence retrieval + safety monitor + final human decision. A similar structure could be useful for computational genomics, genomic prediction, GWAS interpretation, literature review, and automated method benchmarking.

Computational Genomics

2. UCLA Computational Genomics videos: very useful, but hard to keep watching

The UCLA Computational Genomics Summer Institute video archive is a very useful resource for computational genomics, statistical genetics, genomics technology, genomic biobanks, machine learning in health, and related topics.

Main challenge: The resource is valuable, but the real difficulty is consistency. It is easy to bookmark the page and never return. The lab needs a lightweight watching system.

A practical watching strategy

  • One video per week: choose one talk that matches the lab’s current research direction.
  • One-page summary: whoever watches the video writes a short summary with three points: method, dataset, and relevance to our work.
  • Five-minute lab sharing: present only the useful ideas, not a full lecture summary.
  • Tag by topic: for example, “single-cell,” “genomic prediction,” “biobank,” “machine learning,” “privacy,” or “foundation model.”

For students, this could become a low-pressure way to build background knowledge. For the lab, it can become a shared knowledge base rather than a long list of unwatched videos.

AI for Science

3. Three Nature papers: automating scientific discovery using AI

The attached image highlights three recent Nature papers, all published online on May 19, 2026. Together, they signal a major shift: AI systems are now being framed not just as writing assistants, but as systems that can help generate hypotheses, coordinate scientific workflows, and write expert-level research software.

Image note: three Nature accelerated article previews related to AI systems for scientific discovery, Co-Scientist, and empirical scientific software.
A multi-agent system for automating scientific discovery
Nature, published online May 19, 2026. DOI: 10.1038/s41586-026-10652-y

This paper represents the “multi-agent scientist” direction: instead of one chatbot answering one question, multiple specialized agents can divide the work of scientific discovery into roles such as planning, literature review, hypothesis generation, experiment design, coding, analysis, and critique.

Accelerating scientific discovery with Co-Scientist
Nature, published online May 19, 2026. DOI: 10.1038/s41586-026-10644-y

This paper fits the “AI as scientific collaborator” direction. The important question is not whether AI can replace scientists, but whether it can help scientists generate, rank, refine, and test better hypotheses faster.

An AI system to help scientists write expert-level empirical software
Nature, published online May 19, 2026. DOI: 10.1038/s41586-026-10658-6

This paper focuses on a very practical bottleneck in science: many scientific ideas require high-quality empirical software before they can be tested. If AI can help turn a research objective into robust code and evaluation, it can accelerate the entire research cycle.

Big picture: These three papers can be viewed as three layers of AI for science:
  1. Co-Scientist: AI helps generate and refine hypotheses.
  2. Multi-agent discovery: AI helps coordinate scientific workflows.
  3. Empirical software generation: AI helps implement and test scientific ideas.

Why this matters for quantitative genetics and G2P

For our field, the opportunity is to build AI systems that do not only summarize papers or write code. A more ambitious direction is an AI-assisted research loop:

  • read relevant genetics and genomics literature,
  • identify a modeling gap or biological question,
  • propose a statistical or machine-learning method,
  • implement the method,
  • benchmark it on real and simulated datasets,
  • interpret the results biologically,
  • and prepare a reproducible report or manuscript draft.

This is especially relevant for genome-to-phenome prediction, functional annotation, foundation models, longitudinal phenotypes, and cross-species genomic prediction, where the bottleneck is not one single step but the coordination of many steps.
