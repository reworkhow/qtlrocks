AI Agents

1. Karpathy: when LLM coding agents fail

Andrej Karpathy shared a useful set of observations on how LLMs fail when writing code. The recurring pattern is not just that the model makes mistakes. The deeper problem is that it often makes silent assumptions, overbuilds, edits unrelated code, and declares success before the work has actually been verified.

Main signal: Good agent behavior is mostly disciplined problem solving: understand the request, keep the solution small, avoid unrelated changes, and verify the result before moving on.

The practical rules

Think before coding

State assumptions clearly. If the task has multiple interpretations, surface them instead of guessing silently.

Simplicity first

Use the minimum code that solves the actual problem. Avoid speculative abstractions and unnecessary flexibility.

Surgical changes

Touch only what the task requires. Do not clean up, rewrite, or reformat nearby code unless asked.

Goal-driven execution

Turn the task into something verifiable, then loop until the evidence says the job is done.

My takeaway: This is also great for general problem solving. State the goal, avoid vague assumptions, keep the solution focused, and test whether the result actually works.

Startup Thinking

2. Andrew Ng: concrete ideas create speed

In his talk Building Faster with AI, Andrew Ng makes a sharp point about startup ideas. Vague ideas often get praise because they are hard to disagree with. But that same vagueness makes them hard to build.

"When you're vague, you're almost always right. But when you're concrete, you may be right or wrong."

Ng's point is that concrete ideas give teams a direction. Once the idea is specific enough, a team can build it, test it, and decide whether to keep going or reject it.

Why this matters: Being concrete introduces the risk of being wrong, but it also creates the possibility of learning quickly. Vague ideas feel safe because they avoid contact with reality.
My takeaway: This is the line worth remembering: when you are vague, you are almost always right. When you are concrete, you may be right or wrong. That is exactly why concrete thinking is useful.

Aging + Transcriptomics

3. Gene expression as an aging and mortality signal

A Nature News & Views article discusses work showing that large-scale RNA transcript patterns across rodents, monkeys, and humans can be used to estimate chronological age and predict mortality risk. The study points to transcriptomic signatures that are shared across tissues and mammalian species.

Main signal: Gene-expression data may provide a measurable molecular clock for aging, which could help evaluate healthspan and lifespan interventions faster than waiting for long-term outcomes.

Why this is interesting for the lab

  • Measurement: aging becomes something that can be estimated from molecular data, not only observed over time.
  • Translation: shared signals across species may help connect model-organism studies with human biology.
  • Prediction: transcriptomic patterns could become useful features for risk, age, and intervention-response models.
My takeaway: Biological age is becoming more measurable. For quantitative genetics and genomics, the important question is how these molecular clocks connect with genotype, environment, tissue context, and phenotype.
