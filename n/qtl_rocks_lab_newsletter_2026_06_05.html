Writing + Iteration

1. First drafts can be garbage, and that is okay

The attached image is useful because it shows both the idea and the process: a first draft can be garbage, and revision is how we make it better. The exact attribution matters less than the habit: make a rough first version, then improve it.

The first draft of anything is garbage.
The useful part of a first draft is that it turns an idea into something we can revise.
Main signal: Do not wait for the first version to be beautiful. Make the first version concrete, then use revision, feedback, and validation to improve it.

Why this matters for the lab

Many research tasks stay stuck because they are still abstract: an idea for a paper, a possible analysis, a method that might work, or a paragraph that has not been written yet. A rough draft changes the situation. Once there is something on the page or something running in code, we can criticize it, test it, and improve it.

My takeaway: AI is useful because it can help us get from zero to first draft faster. But the real value is not the first output. The real value comes from editing, checking, testing, and improving the draft until it becomes useful.
AI for Research

2. Jiayi reduced computing time from two days to three hours

Jiayi reduced a research computation from about two days to about three hours. This is a very good example of using AI for research because it directly improves the research feedback loop.

Before
2 days
After
3 hours
Main signal: AI is not only useful for writing text or generating code. It becomes especially valuable when it shortens the time between trying an idea and seeing whether the idea works.

This matters because revision timelines are short. If a project has only six months for revision, but an important computation is slow, the team cannot test enough ideas. A two-day run makes feedback expensive. A three-hour run makes iteration much more realistic.

What makes this kind of AI help work

A well-defined question

The problem needs to be specific enough that AI can help improve the workflow instead of wandering around a vague research goal.

An easy validation approach

The team needs a clear way to check whether the faster method still gives the correct result.

Energy

Someone has to keep pushing through the boring parts: debugging, testing, profiling, and rerunning.

Motivation

The motivation can come from curiosity, ambition, enjoyment, pressure, or a deadline. The important thing is having enough drive to finish.

Why this matters: When computation is slow, research feels stuck because every mistake costs too much time. When computation is fast, mistakes become easier to learn from, and the team can improve the work much more quickly.
My takeaway: The best use of AI in research is not magic. It is concrete. Start with a well-defined question, make validation easy, then use AI to remove the bottleneck that is slowing down feedback.
Career + Lab Life

3. Networking is a research skill, not a personality test

A Nature Careers column by Caroline Dunne offers practical advice for scientists who dislike conference networking. The useful framing is that networking does not have to mean walking into a room and trying to impress everyone. It can be treated as a structured professional skill.

Main signal: Networking becomes easier when it is concrete: identify a few people, prepare a real question, have a short conversation, and follow up after the meeting.

A practical conference strategy

  • Before the meeting: choose a few people you want to meet and read one abstract or paper from each person.
  • During the meeting: ask one specific question instead of trying to make a generic impression.
  • After the meeting: send a short follow-up message that reminds them what you discussed.
Why this matters: Conferences are not only for listening to talks. They are also where students and researchers learn what people are actually working on, make their own work visible, and find future collaborators.
My takeaway: My personal approach to networking is simple: always show up and stay present. That means consistently being there, staying engaged, and maintaining a continuous presence in your professional circles. You do not need to be the loudest person in the room. You need to keep showing up, keep asking thoughtful questions, keep following up, and let people remember that you are serious, reliable, and part of the conversation.
