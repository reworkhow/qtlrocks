AI Learning

1. Transformer Explainer: making LLM internals visible

Transformer Explainer is an interactive tool from the Polo Club team that helps people understand how transformer models work. Instead of treating an LLM as a mysterious black box, the tool lets users inspect the steps that connect input text to next-token prediction.

Tokens text becomes model-readable pieces
Embeddings tokens become vectors
Attention tokens look at other tokens
MLP features are transformed
Output the model predicts what comes next
Main signal: Interactive explanations make abstract AI concepts easier to inspect. This is especially useful for students who use LLMs every day but still need to understand what is happening underneath.

Why this matters for the lab

We should not only learn how to prompt AI. We should also understand the model well enough to know what it can and cannot do. Visual tools make it easier to ask better questions about attention, probability, temperature, top-k, top-p, and why the model produces one answer instead of another.

My takeaway: The best AI training combines use and understanding. Tools like Transformer Explainer turn the abstract parts of transformers into something concrete, which makes it easier to use AI responsibly and teach it to new students.

Medical Education

2. AI-induced never-skilling in medical education

A Nature Medicine perspective argues that medical education now faces a new AI-related risk: not only deskilling, where experienced people lose abilities, but never-skilling, where trainees rely on AI so early that they never fully develop foundational clinical reasoning.

Main signal: AI can make learners faster, but speed is not the same as competence. If trainees outsource reasoning too early, they may produce correct-looking answers without developing independent judgment.

Why this matters beyond medicine

This is also a research-training problem. In statistics, genomics, coding, and paper writing, students can now ask AI to generate polished answers before they understand the core concepts. That can help productivity, but it can also hide weak foundations.

My takeaway: AI should not replace the painful but necessary stage of learning. We need baseline competency first, then AI-assisted learning. The order matters: learn to think, then use AI to think faster and better.

Biology + Navigation

3. Pigeon navigation and superparamagnetic macrophages

A Science paper reports that homing pigeon navigation under overcast conditions relies on superparamagnetic macrophages. The surprising part is the proposed link between navigation behavior, iron-rich immune cells, liver biology, and magnetosensation.

Main signal: Biology can connect systems that look unrelated at first. A navigation phenotype may involve immune cells and liver tissue, not only the obvious sensory organs.

Why this is interesting for the lab

  • Mechanism can be surprising: the most important tissue or cell type may not be where we first expect it.
  • Behavior needs systems thinking: navigation connects environment, physiology, cells, and neural pathways.
  • Broad reading matters: unusual papers can train us to notice connections across fields.
My takeaway: This is exactly why reading broadly matters. Sometimes the most interesting scientific ideas come from connections that look strange at first: immune cells as sensors, liver as compass, and behavior explained by cell biology. Good research often starts with being surprised.
